DFM for Machined and Fabricated Parts: Setup Reduction and Material Choice

DFM for machined and fabricated parts focuses on making the part easier to produce by reducing setups, fixturing, and the need for more practical material selection. The main goal is to reduce machining time and complexity while maintaining the part's strength, accuracy, and suitability for its function.

Why Machined Part DFM Matters

Machined parts often become expensive because of excessive re-fixturing, complex tool paths, and over-specified materials. A design that requires many setups usually increases labor time, raises the chance of error, and makes consistency harder to control.

Fabricated parts face a similar issue when the geometry forces extra cutting, welding, or secondary operations. Good DFM keeps the part as simple as possible for the shop process that will produce it.

Reduce the Number of Setups

One of the most important DFM principles for machining is to reduce the number of setups. Every time a part must be removed, re-clamped, or reoriented, the process takes longer, and the risk of alignment error increases.

When possible, design the part so it can be completed in a single setup or at least with minimal fixturing changes. This is especially important for precision parts where positional accuracy matters.

Keep Cuts in One Direction

Machining from multiple angles can increase cycle time and make the process harder to control. A part that can be machined with straightforward tool access is usually more economical than one that needs special angle changes or complex multi-axis operations.

If the design requires machining in multiple directions, it may be worth simplifying the geometry. In many cases, splitting a complex part into two simpler parts is a better solution than forcing one difficult component.

Choose Materials Wisely

Material selection has a major effect on machinability, cost, and tool wear. Harder materials take longer to machine and may require more robust tooling, greater coolant volume, and tighter process control.

That does not mean the softest material is always best. The correct choice is the one that matches the application without over-specifying performance. A DFM review should always ask whether a less expensive and easier-to-machine material can meet the same functional need.

Design for Fabrication Efficiency

For fabricated parts, geometry should support cutting, welding, bending, and assembly with minimal extra handling. Simple shapes are easier to build accurately and usually require less rework.

Avoid unnecessary joints or welded features unless they are needed for strength, sealing, or structural purposes. Every added fabrication step increases cost and introduces more opportunities for distortion or variation.

Common DFM Issues in Machined and Fabricated Parts

Typical issues include:

  • Too many setups.
  • Deep pockets or hard-to-reach features.
  • Overly tight tolerances.
  • Unnecessary material thickness.
  • Complex geometry that could be simplified.
  • Material choices that are harder to machine than needed.

These issues are often easy to avoid if DFM is done early.
